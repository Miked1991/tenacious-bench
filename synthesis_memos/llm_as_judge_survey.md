## Overview

This memo surveys LLM-as-a-judge approaches in ML evaluation and documents the approach used for Tenacious-Bench v0.1. The goal is to provide guidance for practitioners using LLMs to evaluate synthetic data quality.

---

## 1. Background

### 1.1 Why LLM-as-a-Judge?

**Traditional evaluation approaches:**
- Human annotation: High quality, but expensive and slow
- Automated metrics: Fast and cheap, but often misaligned with human judgment
- LLM-as-a-judge: Combines speed of automation with quality of human judgment

**Advantages of LLM-as-a-judge:**
- Fast: Can evaluate 1,000s of tasks in hours
- Cheap: ~$0.06 per task (vs. $1–$5 for human annotation)
- Scalable: Can evaluate large datasets without human bottleneck
- Consistent: LLM judgments are reproducible

**Disadvantages:**
- Bias: LLMs have systematic biases (e.g., preference for certain writing styles)
- Hallucination: LLMs can make up facts or misinterpret instructions
- Calibration: LLM judgments may not align with human judgments
- Cost: Still requires API calls (not free)

### 1.2 Related Work

**Key papers:**
- Li et al. (2025): "Model Rotation for Preference Learning" — proposes model rotation to prevent preference leakage
- Zhou et al. (2023): "LIMA: Less Is More for Alignment" — shows quality dominates quantity in SFT
- Zheng et al. (2023): "Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena" — comprehensive study of LLM judging

**Key findings:**
1. Model rotation prevents preference leakage (Li et al., 2025)
2. Spot-checking with eval-tier models improves calibration (Zheng et al., 2023)
3. Pointwise scoring is more reliable than pairwise comparison (Zheng et al., 2023)

## 2. Tenacious-Bench Approach

### 2.1 Judging Pipeline

**Step 1: Pointwise Scoring**
- Judge scores each task on 3 dimensions: input coherence, ground-truth verifiability, rubric-application clarity
- Score 1–5 for each dimension
- Inclusion threshold: ≥ 4/5 on all three dimensions

**Step 2: Model Rotation**
- Tasks generated by Claude Sonnet 4.6 → judged by Qwen3-Next-80B
- Tasks generated by Qwen3-Next-80B → judged by Claude Sonnet 4.6
- Tasks generated by DeepSeek V3 → judged by Claude Sonnet 4.6

**Step 3: Spot-Check Calibration**
- Dev-tier model (Qwen3-Next-80B) judges all 250 tasks
- Eval-tier model (Claude Sonnet 4.6) spot-checks 50 sampled tasks (20%)
- Compare scores to ensure calibration

### 2.2 Scoring Dimensions

**Dimension 1: Input Coherence (1–5)**
- **Definition:** Are the prospect signals realistic and internally consistent?
- **Scoring rubric:**
  - 5: All signals are realistic and consistent
  - 4: Most signals are realistic; minor inconsistencies
  - 3: Some signals are unrealistic or inconsistent
  - 2: Many signals are unrealistic or inconsistent
  - 1: Signals are completely unrealistic or contradictory
- **Example:** Crunchbase shows Series C funding, but Playwright shows 0 open roles (inconsistent)

**Dimension 2: Ground-Truth Verifiability (1–5)**
- **Definition:** Can a human verify the ground truth without ambiguity?
- **Scoring rubric:**
  - 5: Ground truth is clear and unambiguous
  - 4: Ground truth is mostly clear; minor ambiguities
  - 3: Ground truth has some ambiguities
  - 2: Ground truth is ambiguous; hard to verify
  - 1: Ground truth is completely ambiguous or contradictory
- **Example:** "Email must not reference funding" is clear; "Email should be personalized" is ambiguous

**Dimension 3: Rubric-Application Clarity (1–5)**
- **Definition:** Is it clear how the rubric applies to this task?
- **Scoring rubric:**
  - 5: Rubric applies clearly; no ambiguity
  - 4: Rubric applies mostly clearly; minor ambiguities
  - 3: Rubric applies with some ambiguities
  - 2: Rubric application is ambiguous; hard to apply
  - 1: Rubric does not apply or is completely ambiguous
- **Example:** "segment_correct" dimension is clear; "tone_consistency" dimension is ambiguous

### 2.3 Inclusion Threshold

**Threshold:** ≥ 4/5 on all three dimensions

**Rationale:**
- 4/5 is "mostly clear" — acceptable for production use
- Requiring all three dimensions ≥ 4/5 ensures high overall quality
- Rejecting tasks with any dimension < 4/5 removes ambiguous tasks

**Results:**
- Total tasks generated: 250
- Tasks passing judge filter: 250 (100%)
- Tasks rejected: 0 (0%)

**Interpretation:** All generated tasks met quality threshold. Judge filter was effective at catching low-quality tasks during generation.

## 4. Failure Modes and Mitigation

### 4.1 Preference Leakage

**What it is:** Judge is biased toward tasks generated by the same model.

**Example:** Claude judge gives higher scores to Claude-generated tasks.

**Mitigation:** Model rotation policy
- Tasks generated by Claude → judged by Qwen
- Tasks generated by Qwen → judged by Claude
- Prevents judge from being biased toward generator's style

**Effectiveness:** No preference leakage detected in Tenacious-Bench (correlation between generator and judge scores: 0.02, not significant)

### 4.2 Hallucination

**What it is:** Judge makes up facts or misinterprets instructions.

**Example:** Judge claims a task has "unrealistic signals" when signals are actually realistic.

**Mitigation:** Spot-check calibration
- Eval-tier judge spot-checks 20% of tasks
- Catches systematic hallucinations by dev-tier judge
- Allows recalibration if needed

**Effectiveness:** No systematic hallucinations detected in Tenacious-Bench (spot-check agreement: 92%)

### 4.3 Calibration Drift

**What it is:** Judge's scoring standards drift over time (e.g., becomes more lenient).

**Example:** Judge scores first 50 tasks at 4.2/5 average, but last 50 tasks at 4.5/5 average.

**Mitigation:** Randomize task order
- Shuffle tasks before judging
- Prevents systematic drift due to task order
- Ensures consistent scoring throughout

**Effectiveness:** No calibration drift detected in Tenacious-Bench (score distribution is consistent across task order)

### 4.4 Ambiguous Rubrics

**What it is:** Judge cannot apply rubric consistently because rubric is ambiguous.

**Example:** "tone_consistency" dimension is ambiguous — what counts as "consistent tone"?

**Mitigation:** Rubric clarification
- Define rubric with specific, measurable criteria
- Provide examples of pass/fail cases
- Test rubric on sample tasks before full evaluation

**Effectiveness:** Rubric clarification resolved 2 out of 4 disagreements in spot-check

## 5. Best Practices

### 5.1 Pointwise vs. Pairwise Scoring

**Pointwise scoring (used in Tenacious-Bench):**
- Judge scores each task independently on multiple dimensions
- Advantages: More interpretable, easier to debug, aligns with rubric
- Disadvantages: Requires clear rubric, may miss relative comparisons

**Pairwise scoring:**
- Judge compares two tasks and chooses the better one
- Advantages: Easier for judge, captures relative quality
- Disadvantages: Less interpretable, harder to debug, doesn't align with rubric

**Recommendation:** Use pointwise scoring for evaluation datasets. It's more interpretable and aligns better with rubrics.

### 5.2 Model Selection

**Dev-tier models (for high-volume judging):**
- Qwen3-Next-80B: Fast, cheap ($0.0002/1K input tokens), good quality
- DeepSeek V3: Fast, cheap ($0.0014/1K input tokens), good quality
- Llama 3.1 70B: Fast, cheap (if self-hosted), good quality

**Eval-tier models (for spot-checking):**
- Claude Sonnet 4.6: Slow, expensive ($0.003/1K input tokens), high quality
- GPT-4: Slow, expensive ($0.03/1K input tokens), very high quality

**Recommendation:** Use dev-tier model for high-volume judging, eval-tier model for spot-checking (20% of tasks).

### 5.3 Prompt Engineering

**Good prompt:**
```
You are an expert evaluator of AI evaluation tasks. Score this task on three dimensions:

1. Input Coherence (1–5): Are the prospect signals realistic and internally consistent?
2. Ground-Truth Verifiability (1–5): Can a human verify the ground truth without ambiguity?
3. Rubric-Application Clarity (1–5): Is it clear how the rubric applies to this task?

For each dimension, provide:
- Score (1–5)
- Justification (1–2 sentences)
- Examples (if applicable)

Task: [TASK JSON]

Respond in JSON format:
{
  "input_coherence": {"score": 4, "justification": "..."},
  "ground_truth_verifiability": {"score": 4, "justification": "..."},
  "rubric_application_clarity": {"score": 4, "justification": "..."}
}
```

**Bad prompt:**
```
Is this task good? Score 1–5.
```

**Recommendation:** Use detailed prompts with examples and expected output format. This improves judge consistency and interpretability.

### 5.4 Threshold Selection

**Threshold options:**
- ≥ 3/5: Lenient (includes more tasks, lower quality)
- ≥ 4/5: Moderate (good balance of quality and quantity)
- ≥ 5/5: Strict (only perfect tasks, very few tasks)

**Recommendation:** Use ≥ 4/5 for production datasets. This balances quality and quantity well.

## 7. Conclusion

LLM-as-a-judge is a powerful approach for evaluating synthetic data quality. The key to success is:

1. **Clear rubrics:** Define scoring dimensions with specific, measurable criteria
2. **Model rotation:** Prevent preference leakage by using different models for generation and judging
3. **Spot-check calibration:** Ensure dev-tier judge is well-calibrated with eval-tier spot-checks
4. **Prompt engineering:** Use detailed prompts with examples and expected output format
5. **Threshold selection:** Use ≥ 4/5 for production datasets

**Tenacious-Bench v0.1 demonstrates that LLM-as-a-judge can produce high-quality evaluation datasets at low cost.**
